01 / The Gap

Demand far exceeds supply

Routt County doesn't have a child care preference problem. It has a child care supply crisis — with practically no access available for infants.

0%
of families with infants and toddlers cannot find care in Routt County1,2

The shortage is most severe for infants. There are just 51 licensed infant slots in Routt County, against an estimated need of 300 — a gap of 249. Toddlers face a similar crisis: 98 licensed slots against 318 needed. Universal Preschool (UPK) expansion has brought preschool closer to parity, but no comparable support exists for children under three. For working parents of babies and toddlers, the math simply doesn't work.

This isn't a budgeting problem for families. It's a market failure. The true cost of quality infant care — $25,000–$35,000 per child per year — far exceeds what any family can reasonably pay. Families in Routt County are already stretched to the breaking point, spending three to four times the federal affordability threshold. And even that sacrifice isn't enough to make the system work.

03 / The Workforce

Educators can't afford to live here on what they earn

The people who care for Routt County's youngest children are paid wages that don't cover the basic cost of living — in one of Colorado's most expensive communities.

$3/hr
less than what it takes to simply live in Routt County — the gap between the median educator wage ($26/hr) and the MIT Living Wage ($29/hr)3,4
Education is their passion — a second job is their reality

Sources: 3 First Impressions Wage Survey, July 2025 (N=7 providers)   4 MIT Living Wage Calculator, 2023 (single adult, no children, Routt County)

High turnover isn't a sign that educators don't care — it's a sign that the wages don't work. When a lead teacher earning $21–$27 per hour can't cover rent in a county where the living wage is $29/hour, they leave. And every time a teacher leaves, children lose continuity of care, families lose stability, and providers lose the investment they made in training and onboarding.

04 / The Ripple Effects

Child care failure is driving families out of Routt County

The effects of the child care crisis extend far beyond individual families. When families can't afford child care, housing, and livable wages all at once — they leave. The community is losing the workforce it needs, and the economic consequences are mounting.

residents ages 25–44 lost since the 2018 peak — a steady seven-year decline while Routt County's total population held essentially flat5

The economic cost of inaction — and the case for investment

Sources: 1 Strategic Investment Plan (2026)   2 Brodsky Report (2025)   6 Eagle County Lodging Tax, Roaring Fork Valley Quarter-cent Sales Tax, Larimer County local mechanism   7 Economic modeling, Routt County

The trendline tells the story: Routt County's 25–44 population peaked in 2018 at 7,452 and has declined every measured year since — even as the county's total population stayed flat. These are the families who need child care, and they are leaving. While peer mountain resort communities have created dedicated local funding mechanisms to keep families here, Routt County has none. Eagle County and Roaring Fork Valley are investing $8–10 million per year. Routt County is investing zero.
